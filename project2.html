Equitable Access to Green Space in Boston

Environmental Justice Census Data GIS Spatial Join
Problem
Roxbury and Dorchester have the most parks in Boston — yet their residents are among the least well-served. How can raw park counts mislead, and what does per-capita access reveal?
Methods
Spatially joined the City of Boston Parks Dataset to neighborhood boundary layers (WGS 84), then calculated a parks-per-1,000-residents metric using 2020 Census data. Visualized as two choropleth maps (Map A: raw count; Map B: per capita) using graduated colors and natural breaks to expose the gap between quantity and access.
Key Finding
Roxbury (144 parks) and Dorchester (143) top the raw count — but high population density means residents still experience overcrowding and limited access. Suburban neighborhoods like West Roxbury and Hyde Park score far higher per capita. Downtown, Leather District, and Harbor Islands have zero parks per capita.
Policy Implication
Raw park counts are a misleading equity metric. Per-capita access, tied to population density and demographics, should guide capital investment. Priority targets: Leather District, Charlestown, and Downtown for new green space; Roxbury and Dorchester for transit access and infrastructure upgrades.
