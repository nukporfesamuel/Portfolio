Project 01

Housing Preservation vs. Redevelopment in Boston

Parcel-Level GISRegressionCommunity-BasedHousing Policy
Problem
When money flows into Boston neighborhoods, does it strengthen homes people already rely on - or replace them? Community partners at the Dudley Street Neighborhood Initiative raised this question directly during workshops.
Methods
Constructed a custom Preservation Ratio (renovation $ ÷ total investment $) from 691,985 building permits across 2,146 census blocks. Applied hierarchical permit classification, corporate ownership detection, and multivariate regression with an interaction term to test the Blue Hill Avenue corridor specifically.
Key Finding
Demographics (race, income, location) do not predict preservation. Corporate ownership does - but with a striking reversal on Blue Hill Ave: where corporate owners drive redevelopment citywide (β = −0.329, p < 0.001), the same ownership correlates with higher preservation on Blue Hill (interaction β = +0.926, p < 0.05).
Policy Implication
High preservation ratios don't guarantee stability - renovations can displace through rent increases just as effectively as demolition. Policy must address both pathways: not just preventing demolition, but pairing renovation with rent stabilization and affordability covenants.

Project 02

Equitable Access to Green Space in Boston

Environmental JusticeCensus DataGISSpatial Join
Problem
Roxbury and Dorchester have the most parks in Boston - yet their residents are among the least well-served. How can raw park counts mislead, and what does per-capita access reveal?
Methods
Spatially joined the City of Boston Parks Dataset to neighborhood boundary layers (WGS 84), then calculated a parks-per-1,000-residents metric using 2020 Census data. Visualized as two choropleth maps (Map A: raw count; Map B: per capita) using graduated colors and natural breaks to expose the gap between quantity and access.
Key Finding
Roxbury (144 parks) and Dorchester (143) top the raw count - but high population density means residents still experience overcrowding and limited access. Suburban neighborhoods like West Roxbury and Hyde Park score far higher per capita. Downtown, Leather District, and Harbor Islands have zero parks per capita.
Policy Implication
Raw park counts are a misleading equity metric. Per-capita access, tied to population density and demographics, should guide capital investment. Priority targets: Leather District, Charlestown, and Downtown for new green space; Roxbury and Dorchester for transit access and infrastructure upgrades to reduce overcrowding pressure.

Project 03

TB Burden by Country: Visualizing Global Tuberculosis Trends

R · ggplot2TableauPublic HealthWHO Data
Problem
How does TB burden vary across WHO regions over time - and what is the relationship between prevalence, HIV co-infection, and case detection rates?
Methods
Cleaned and analyzed Tableau's public "TB Burden by Country" dataset using R (ggplot2, kableExtra, mosaic). Built a multi-chart analysis: bar charts of average regional prevalence, boxplots of within-region distribution, a histogram of global prevalence skew, a time-series of HIV+ TB incidence (1990–2010), and a scatter plot of prevalence vs. HIV co-infection. Extended analysis with a 6-panel Tableau Story covering regional distribution, disparity, co-infection, global trends, and burden relationships.
Key Finding
Africa (avg ~375/100k) and South-East Asia (~390/100k) carry the highest prevalence - far above the Americas (~90/100k) and Europe (~100/100k). HIV+ TB incidence peaked around 2000 then declined sharply. Yet the median global case detection rate is only 69%, meaning nearly 1 in 3 cases goes undetected.
Policy Implication
Visualization choice determines what decision-makers see. Bar charts obscure within-region variation that boxplots reveal; line charts make trend reversals visible that tables hide. Standardizing public health data presentation - choosing chart types deliberately - can change resource allocation priorities.
