TB Burden by Country: Visualizing Global Tuberculosis Trends

R · ggplot2 Tableau Public Health WHO Data
Problem
How does TB burden vary across WHO regions over time - and what is the relationship between prevalence, HIV co-infection, and case detection rates?
Methods
Cleaned and analyzed Tableau's public "TB Burden by Country" dataset using R (ggplot2, kableExtra, mosaic). Built a multi-chart analysis: bar charts of average regional prevalence, boxplots of within-region distribution, a histogram of global prevalence skew, a time-series of HIV+ TB incidence (1990–2010), and a scatter plot of prevalence vs. HIV co-infection. Extended with a 6-panel Tableau Story covering regional distribution, disparity, co-infection, global trends, and burden relationships.
Key Finding
Africa and S-E Asia top average prevalence - but the bigger story is within-region inequality: Africa's range spans 0–1,762/100k while Europe stays near 0–100/100k. The mean detection rate of 65% (SD = 24.8%) masks countries detecting fewer than 10% of cases - a hidden crisis invisible in regional averages alone.
Policy Implication
TB funding and intervention programs should target high-burden countries within regions rather than treating entire WHO regions as uniform. A regional average of 375 per 100k in Africa masks countries ranging from near zero to 1,762 - aggregated funding formulas will systematically under-resource the countries that need it most.

Headline Findings

231.8

Mean TB prevalence per 100,000 globally - heavily skewed by high-burden regions.

65%

Mean case detection rate (SD = 24.8%) - some countries detect fewer than 10% of cases.

1,762

Max TB prevalence per 100k in Africa - vs. ~100 in Europe. Within-region inequality is the real story.

Policy Takeaway

Africa's TB burden is far more unequal than regional averages suggest - within-region variation, not just between-region comparisons, should drive resource allocation.
